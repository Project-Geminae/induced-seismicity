Frequently Asked Questions

How regulators, operators, and researchers use the Induced Seismicity Dashboard to make decisions.

For Regulators

How does a regulator look at an event and use this tool?

When an earthquake occurs (say, an M4.3), the regulator needs to quickly determine: which disposal wells contributed, how much, and what action to take. Here's the step-by-step:

1. Open the dashboard. All TexNet earthquakes are on the map.
2. Click the earthquake. The sidebar shows the event detail: location, magnitude, depth, date.
3. Set the radius slider (default 7 km). This controls how far from the epicenter to search for disposal wells.
4. The WELL ATTRIBUTION panel populates with every SWD well in that radius, ranked by causal contribution (CATE).
5. Look at the CATE waterfall chart. Each bar is a well's estimated causal contribution to the earthquake's magnitude. The model controls for geology (fault distance, fault count, depth, days active) AND what neighboring wells are injecting (spatial interference).
6. Check the 95% confidence intervals. Wells where the CI excludes zero are statistically significant contributors. Wells where the CI crosses zero are ambiguous — inconclusive is not the same as innocent.
7. Click the top contributing well's card to see its threshold curve — this shows exactly what volume cap would bring that well's contribution below the regulatory limit.
8. Click "↓ Download Report" in the attribution summary to generate a timestamped PDF with the full per-well CATE table, charts, CIs, and methodology citation — ready for regulatory filings.
Decision rule: If the top 3-5 wells have CATEs with CIs excluding zero, issue targeted volume reduction orders using the threshold curve. If every well's CI crosses zero (distributed causation), look at the TMLE population panel and consider an area-wide volume reduction instead.

How does a regulator use this tool for regulation approvals of SWDs?

When an operator applies for a new SWD permit or requests a volume increase, the regulator can use the dashboard to assess the risk profile of the area:

  1. Check the map for historical seismicity near the proposed well location. Filter by magnitude and year range to see trends.
  2. Click nearby events to see which existing wells were attributed as contributors. If wells near the proposed location have consistently high CATEs, the area is high-risk.
  3. Check the TMLE population panel at the bottom. The dose-response curve shows how expected seismicity changes with volume at this radius. If the curve is steep in the volume range the operator is requesting, the marginal risk is high.
  4. Use the threshold curve for existing nearby wells to understand what volume levels are already producing significant CATEs. If the proposed well would add to an area that's already near the threshold, tighter conditions are warranted.
  5. Check depth class. Wells injecting below 10,000 ft (tagged DEEP) are closer to basement faults and have systematically different risk profiles than shallow wells. Depth-based conditions may be appropriate.
For permit conditions: The threshold curve provides a defensible, well-specific volume cap. Instead of a blanket "no more than X BBL/day" for the entire area, the regulator can set conditions based on the actual causal model: "Volume shall not exceed Y BBL cumulative 365-day, corresponding to a CATE below 0.03 ML at the 7 km radius."

What if the dashboard shows small CATEs for a large earthquake?

This is the distributed causation scenario. It means injection did contribute to the earthquake (the population TMLE confirms it), but the causal effect is spread across many wells — each contributing an undetectably small amount. No individual well pushed the fault past its threshold alone; they all did, collectively, over months or years of cumulative pressure buildup.

In this case, targeted shut-ins are not supported by the data. The right tool is the TMLE shift analysis in the population panel: "If all wells within R km reduce volume by 10%, expected magnitude drops by X." That's the basis for an area-wide volume reduction order.

How do I explain this to a judge or hearing officer?

The methodology uses established statistical frameworks (TMLE: van der Laan & Rose 2011; Causal Forest: Athey, Tibshirani & Wager 2019). Key points for legal settings:

  • Doubly robust: The estimate is valid if either the outcome model or the treatment model is correctly specified. It doesn't require both to be perfect. The SuperLearner uses 4 diverse algorithms (Ridge, GBM, XGBoost, RandomForest) with an NNLS metalearner ensuring proper convex combination of weights.
  • Confidence intervals: Every CATE comes with a 95% CI derived from honest sample splitting (not bootstrapping). CIs use Bessel-corrected cluster-robust influence-function standard errors. A CI excluding zero means statistical significance at the 5% level.
  • Validated: The Python TMLE implementation was cross-checked against the gold-standard R tlverse ecosystem and agreed to within 0.06%.
  • Conservative: The Causal Forest uses honest estimation — separate data for building the tree structure vs estimating effects — with 5-fold cross-validation.
  • Spatial interference: The model controls for total injection from all neighboring wells within 7 km, so the CATE for well A is not contaminated by what well B next door is doing. This addresses a key critique in the spatial causal inference literature.
  • Sensitivity tested: W×M interaction (p=0.91, mediation decomposition valid), formation sensitivity (indirect pathway robust), positivity diagnostics (max H=4.8, targeting step stable), injection rate multicollinearity (tested and rejected, VIF=5.5).
  • Downloadable report: Every analysis can be exported as a timestamped PDF with full per-well table, charts, and methodology citations.

For Operators

How does an operator look at an event and use this tool?

After an earthquake near your well, you need to know: did my well contribute, and if so, how much? Here's how to find out:

1. Open the dashboard and click the earthquake.
2. Find your well in the WELL ATTRIBUTION list (sorted by contribution, highest first).
3. Check your well's CATE value and 95% CI.
4. If the CI crosses zero → the data does not show statistically significant evidence that your well contributed. This is a defensible position.
5. If the CI excludes zero → the model estimates your well contributed. Click your well card to see the threshold curve and determine what volume would bring your CATE below the regulatory limit.
Compliance defense: A well with CATE = +0.01 ML and CI [-0.04, +0.06] has a confidence interval crossing zero. This means there is no statistically significant evidence at the 95% level that this well contributed to the earthquake. This is a quantitative, scientifically defensible argument — not just "trust me."

How does an operator use this tool for planning?

Before the next earthquake happens, you can use the dashboard to manage your fleet's seismic risk proactively:

  1. Review historical events near your wells. Click past earthquakes near your disposal wells and check if your wells have been attributed as significant contributors (CIs excluding zero).
  2. Check threshold curves for your highest-risk wells. Click each well card to see its dose-response curve. The threshold curve tells you: at what volume does my CATE cross the regulatory limit? If you're operating at 3M BBL and the threshold is at 800K BBL, you have a risk gap.
  3. Compare wells across your fleet. Which of your wells have the highest CATEs for nearby events? These are your highest-risk assets. Consider:
    • Reducing volume at the highest-risk wells first (most risk reduction per barrel lost)
    • Shifting volume to lower-risk wells (shallow, far from faults)
    • Monitoring the depth class — DEEP wells (>10,000 ft) near basement carry more risk
  4. Check the population dose-response curve. The TMLE curve shows how expected seismicity changes with volume at different levels. If your well is operating in the steep part of the curve, small volume reductions give large risk reductions.
  5. Use this for new well planning. Before drilling a new SWD well, check historical seismicity and attribution patterns in the area. Deep wells near active fault zones with high historical CATEs are the highest-risk locations.
Portfolio optimization: If you operate 30 SWD wells and need to reduce total disposal by 15%, the threshold curves tell you which wells to throttle first for the most seismic risk reduction per barrel of lost capacity. Throttle the wells at the top of the CATE rankings and leave the low-risk wells at full capacity.

My well shows a negative CATE. What does that mean?

A negative CATE means the Causal Forest estimates that your well's injection may have slightly reduced stress on this particular fault geometry. This can happen physically — injecting in one location can redistribute stress away from a nearby fault, similar to how pressing down on one side of a surface can relieve pressure elsewhere.

A negative CATE does not mean your well is "helping." It means for this specific earthquake, on this specific fault, the net effect was stress reduction. The same well could have a positive CATE for a different earthquake on a different fault.

Can I use this to defend against a blanket shut-in order?

Yes, but carefully. If the regulator orders a blanket shut-in of all wells within R km, and your well has a CATE with CI crossing zero, you have quantitative evidence that your specific well did not contribute significantly. However:

  • A CI crossing zero doesn't prove zero contribution — it means the data is insufficient to distinguish your well's effect from noise.
  • In a distributed-causation scenario (many small CATEs), an area-wide reduction may be warranted even though no individual well is significant. The collective effect is real.
  • The strength of your argument depends on the CI width. A narrow CI near zero is stronger than a wide CI that happens to cross zero.

Understanding the Data

What does CATE mean?

Conditional Average Treatment Effect. It answers: "How much higher is the expected maximum earthquake magnitude because this well injected the volume it did, compared to a counterfactual where it injected nothing?"

A CATE of +0.05 ML means this well's injection is estimated to have added 0.05 to the expected maximum magnitude within the search radius on the event date. It is a causal estimate, not a correlation — it controls for fault distance, fault segment count, perforation depth, days active, depth class (shallow/mid/deep), AND total injection from all neighboring wells within 7 km (spatial interference). This last confounder is critical: it means the CATE for well A reflects well A's own contribution, not the effect of the whole cluster around it.

What does the 95% confidence interval mean?

We're 95% sure the true CATE is somewhere between the lower and upper bound. If the interval includes zero, we cannot be confident the well contributed at all. If it excludes zero, we're statistically confident it did.

A narrow CI (e.g., [+0.03, +0.07]) means we have precise knowledge. A wide CI (e.g., [-0.06, +0.12]) means the data is noisy and we're uncertain. Width depends on how much data is available for wells with similar characteristics.

What is the threshold curve?

For a specific well, the threshold curve shows how the CATE changes as you vary the well's injection volume (holding everything else constant). It's a per-well dose-response curve.

The red dashed horizontal line is the regulatory threshold (default 0.03 ML). Where the CATE curve crosses the threshold line tells you the maximum allowable volume for that well. The cyan vertical line shows the well's current operating volume.

If the current volume line is to the right of the threshold crossing point, the well is operating above the limit. The gap between them is how much volume needs to be reduced.

What's the difference between the per-well CATE and the population TMLE?

The population TMLE (bottom panel) gives the average causal effect across all wells and all events at a given radius. It answers: "On average, how much does injection affect seismicity?" This is useful for area-wide policy.

The per-well CATE (Causal Forest, sidebar) gives each individual well's causal contribution to a specific earthquake. It answers: "How much did THIS well contribute to THIS earthquake?" This is useful for targeted action.

Think of it like medicine: the population effect tells you "this drug lowers blood pressure on average." The individual effect tells you "for this patient, with their specific genetics and health history, the drug will lower blood pressure by X."

What does "doubly robust" mean?

Both TMLE and the Causal Forest fit two separate models: an outcome model Q (predicting earthquake magnitude from volume + confounders) and a treatment model g (predicting injection volume from confounders alone). "Doubly robust" means: if either model is correctly specified, the causal estimate is unbiased. You don't need both to be perfect — just one.

In practice: the outcome model uses a SuperLearner with 4 base learners (Ridge regression, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost, RandomForest) combined via NNLS (non-negative least squares) metalearner. The treatment density model uses a ~140-bin histogram with XGBoost multinomial classifier. Both are cross-validated (5-fold). This diversity means even if one algorithm fails, the others compensate — and doubly-robust protection means the overall estimate stays valid.

What data goes into this?

All data comes from public sources:

  • Earthquakes: TexNet Seismic Monitoring Program catalog (University of Texas Bureau of Economic Geology). 7,424 quality-filtered events, April 2026. Updated daily from IRIS FDSN web service via automated ingestion.
  • Injection: Railroad Commission of Texas (RRC) H-10 injection reports. 1,056 SWD wells, 903,887 well-day observations. Updated when TexNet publishes new Google Drive snapshots (checked daily).
  • Faults: Horne et al. (2023) Midland Basin basement fault segment map (~32,000 segments at ~1 km each).

Derived features:

  • Cumulative injection volumes at 30/90/180/365-day lookback windows
  • Volume-weighted average wellhead pressure + depth-corrected BHP (0.45 psi/ft hydrostatic gradient)
  • Nearest fault distance (km) and fault segment count within search radius
  • Depth-class proxy (shallow <6K ft / mid 6-10K ft / deep >10K ft)
  • Neighbor well volume — total cum_vol_365d from all SWD wells within 7 km (spatial interference confounder)

Why don't you use injection formation as a variable?

The operator-reported "Current Injection Formations" field in RRC records is self-reported and not validated. We found inconsistencies where operators reported different formations for wells at similar depths. Instead, we use measured perforation depth (which the RRC does verify) binned into three classes: shallow (<6,000 ft), mid (6,000-10,000 ft), and deep (>10,000 ft). This captures the same physical distinction — shallow carbonate vs. basement-coupled — without trusting potentially wrong labels.

How do you model pressure? The Midland Basin has strong stress anisotropy — how do you account for that?

This is an important question. The Midland Basin is under NW-SE maximum horizontal stress (Shmax), which creates natural fracture networks oriented NE-SW (perpendicular to Shmax). Pore-pressure diffusion is not isotropic — it follows these fracture corridors preferentially, meaning a well might affect a fault 5 km away along the fracture trend but have little effect on a fault 3 km away perpendicular to it.

What the current model does: The spatiotemporal join uses a simple Euclidean radius (BallTree query). It treats the search area as a circle, not an ellipse aligned with Shmax. The Causal Forest then learns within that radius which wells matter most, using fault distance and depth as features — so it can partially capture directional effects (wells aligned with the fracture trend toward a fault will have smaller fault distances), but it doesn't explicitly model anisotropic pressure diffusion.

What this means in practice:

  • The circular radius is a conservative approximation. It includes some wells that may not actually contribute (cross-fracture direction) and gives them small or zero CATEs, which is the correct behavior.
  • The fault distance confounder partially compensates — wells aligned with the fracture trend toward a fault have shorter effective hydraulic distances, which correlates with Euclidean distance along that trend.
  • The Causal Forest is nonparametric and learns interactions between features. If wells at a certain bearing relative to a fault consistently have higher CATEs, the forest will learn this pattern without needing an explicit anisotropy model.

Known limitation: An explicit anisotropic diffusion model — using an elliptical search area aligned with Shmax, or incorporating permeability tensors from geomechanical models — would be more physically motivated. This is a planned enhancement. The data inputs exist (Shmax orientation is well-constrained across the Midland Basin from wellbore breakout and drilling-induced fracture data), but integrating them into the spatiotemporal join requires replacing the BallTree with a directional kernel.

Bottom line: The current model uses a circular radius (isotropic) as a conservative approximation. The Causal Forest learns directional patterns implicitly from the data. An explicit anisotropic diffusion model aligned with Shmax is the logical next step and would sharpen per-well attribution along fracture corridors.

Technical

What radius should I use?

The default of 7 km is a good starting point. The TMLE shift analysis found statistically significant causal effects at 17 of 20 radii (4–20 km). Radii 1–3 km are not significant due to limited sample size (few wells that close to events).

  • 1-3 km: Very local. Few wells, limited statistical power. TMLE shift not significant at these radii. Per-well CATEs may be large but noisy.
  • 4-10 km: The sweet spot for most regulatory decisions. Strong per-well signal, statistically significant population effects, balances near-field mechanical and far-field pressure-mediated pathways.
  • 11-20 km: Captures far-field pressure diffusion. More wells, larger population effect, but individual CATEs get smaller. The effect is predominantly pressure-mediated at these distances.

Is the Causal Forest available at all radii?

Yes. Causal Forest models are trained and deployed at all 20 radii (1–20 km). Per-well CATE attribution, confidence intervals, and threshold curves are available at every radius. All models include the spatial interference confounder (neighbor_cum_vol_7km). Population TMLE estimates (shift, dose-response, mediation) are also available at all 20 radii with the v3 methodology (NNLS metalearner, 5-fold CV, data-adaptive density bins, H-truncation, Bessel-corrected SEs).

Why is part of the dose-response chart shaded red?

The red-shaded region marks extrapolation beyond the training support. The TMLE dose-response is fit on the distribution of cumulative 365-day injection volumes actually observed in the Permian Basin:
  • < 104 BBL: 96.5% of wells
  • 104–106 BBL: well-supported range
  • 107 BBL: only 0.7% of wells inject this much (P99+)
  • 108 BBL: zero observations — pure model extrapolation

Estimates in the red zone should be treated as indicative of curve shape, not as reliable point estimates. If a regulator is considering a volume threshold above ~10 million BBL, the model is extrapolating beyond the data and confidence intervals underestimate true uncertainty.

Tail trimming: The TMLE shift and dose-response now trim the bottom and top 1% of the cumulative-volume distribution before fitting. This prevents the clever covariate H = g(A_pre|L)/g(A|L) from exploding at the edges (positivity diagnostics had shown max H of 215–687 across radii, meaning a few outliers were dominating the targeting step). The trimmed run drops ~16,000 well-day observations from the 800k+ panel and produces more honest confidence intervals.

Why does the model use depth class instead of injection formation?

Operator-reported "Current Injection Formations" in RRC records are self-reported and sometimes unreliable. We replaced them with a measured depth-class proxy (shallow <6,000 ft, mid 6,000–10,000 ft, deep >10,000 ft). Depth is verified by the RRC on permit submission.

Sensitivity analysis (formation_sensitivity.csv): adding formation one-hot indicators back to the model shifts effect estimates:

  • Total effect: ±6% to +169% across radii
  • Direct effect: ±38% to +955% (flips sign at small radii)
  • Indirect (pressure-mediated) effect: relatively stable at ±7–18%

This means: the indirect pressure pathway is robust to formation specification, but the direct (non-pressure) effect is sensitive. Policy decisions based on population-level dose-response curves are more reliable than decisions based on the direct/indirect decomposition.

Why isn't injection rate included as a confounder?

Sensitivity analysis (rate_definition_check.py) tested three rate definitions as candidate confounders. All three had problems:

  • 365-day rate (cum_vol_365d / days_active): correlation 0.89 with cumulative volume, VIF 5.5. Multicollinear — the Causal Forest produced wildly inflated CATEs (12×) with confidence intervals so wide that no well was statistically significant.
  • 90-day rate (cum_vol_90d / 90): correlation 0.84, VIF 3.7. Same problem, slightly less severe.
  • Recent-to-annual ratio (rate_90d / annual_avg_rate): correlation −0.21, VIF 1.1. Clean orthogonality — but the cumulative volume effect collapses to essentially zero, suggesting this control may be too aggressive.

The fundamental issue: cumulative volume = rate × duration, so adding rate as a confounder is equivalent to controlling for duration, which removes most of the variation we're trying to study.

The current model treats cumulative 365-day volume as the policy-relevant treatment (regulators set BBL caps, not rate caps) and reports the average causal effect across the rate distribution. Effects should be interpreted as: "if a regulator caps cumulative annual volume at X BBL, the expected magnitude reduction is Y ML averaged across operationally typical rate patterns."

How often is the data updated?

Earthquakes: Updated daily. An automated script (auto_ingest.py) fetches new events from the IRIS FDSN web service every morning at 6 AM UTC and merges them into the event catalog. It also checks Google Drive for updated TexNet injection data.

Injection data: Updated when TexNet publishes a new snapshot (typically monthly/quarterly). The auto-ingest script polls Google Drive daily and downloads automatically when a new version is detected.

Models: After new injection data arrives, a full pipeline rerun is needed:

  1. Panel rebuild + spatial join (~10 min with 16 workers)
  2. TMLE shift + dose-response (~4 hours with 16 workers, 5-fold CV, 4 base learners)
  3. Causal Forest retraining at all 20 radii (~10 min with 8 workers)
  4. Docker container rebuild + restart (~1 min)

The dashboard ticker bar shows the data vintage: latest event date, TMLE model date, and Causal Forest model date.

Can I download the data?

The source data (TexNet catalog and RRC injection records) is publicly available. The derived data products (CATE estimates, TMLE results) are computed by the pipeline and served through the API. The API endpoints return JSON that can be consumed programmatically. See the API documentation for endpoint details.

Methodology Details

What is the spatial interference confounder?

Disposal wells within a few kilometers of each other share pressure fields. If well A injects heavily, the pore pressure at well B's location increases too. Our original model assumed each well's effect was independent (the SUTVA assumption). This is physically wrong.

To address this, the model includes neighbor_cum_vol_7km — the total cumulative 365-day injection volume from all other SWD wells within 7 km. This is computed for each well using a BallTree spatial query on well coordinates. VIF = 1.12 (no multicollinearity with the treatment).

With this confounder, the CATE for well A answers: "what is well A's causal contribution, holding the total injection from all nearby wells constant?" This separates the individual well's effect from the cluster's collective pressure contribution.

Impact: adding neighbor volume increased the estimated shift effect by +24% at 7 km, consistent with the expectation that controlling for nearby wells sharpens the per-well estimate.

How does the SuperLearner work?

The SuperLearner is an ensemble machine learning method that combines multiple algorithms to get the best prediction. Instead of choosing one algorithm, it:

  1. Trains 4 different algorithms on the data: Ridge regression (linear), Gradient Boosting (sequential trees), XGBoost (parallel trees), and RandomForest (bagged trees)
  2. Uses 5-fold cross-validation to evaluate each algorithm's performance on held-out data
  3. Assigns weights to each algorithm via NNLS (non-negative least squares), so better algorithms get more weight and no algorithm gets a negative weight
  4. The final prediction is the weighted average of all 4 algorithms

This matters because the SuperLearner is used for both the outcome model Q (predicting earthquake magnitude) and is part of the doubly-robust guarantee: even if some algorithms in the stack are badly wrong, the ensemble adapts. The NNLS metalearner ensures proper convex combination (all weights ≥ 0, sum to 1), which is what the tlverse (R) implementation uses by default.

Can I download a PDF report?

Yes. After selecting an event and viewing its attribution, click "↓ Download Report" in the attribution summary panel. This generates a regulatory-ready PDF with:

  • Event details (ID, date, magnitude, location, depth)
  • CATE waterfall chart (embedded matplotlib rendering)
  • Per-well attribution table (API, distance, CATE, 95% CI, depth, volume, significance flag)
  • Statistically significant contributors highlighted
  • Population TMLE context (TE, NDE, NIE, % mediated)
  • Data vintage and methodology citations
  • Generation timestamp

The PDF opens inline in the browser and can be printed or saved. It's designed to be attached to regulatory filings as a formal record of the causal analysis.

What changed between model versions?

Original (OLS baseline): OLS with DoWhy, 50-iteration bootstrap, Baron-Kenny mediation. Found 20× amplification at 3-4 km but had data bugs (synthetic zeros) and linearity assumption.

V1 (TMLE baseline): Doubly-robust TMLE with HurdleSuperLearner (3 learners, 3-fold CV), histogram density (20 bins), RidgeCV metalearner. Fixed data bugs. 0/20 radii significant (positivity violations, max H=335).

V2 (7 expert fixes): NNLS metalearner, data-adaptive bins (~140), data-adaptive positivity floor, H-truncation at 99th percentile, Bessel-corrected SEs, 5-fold CV, RandomForest added. 17/20 radii significant, max H=5.3.

V3 (current, spatial interference): Added neighbor_cum_vol_7km as confounder (VIF=1.12). Effect +24% at 7 km. Causal Forest retrained at all 20 radii with neighbor volume. All TMLE and CF models now control for what neighboring wells are doing.

What are the known limitations?

  • Isotropic search radius: The spatial join uses a circular (isotropic) radius. The Midland Basin has strong NW-SE stress anisotropy — pressure diffuses preferentially along fracture corridors. An elliptical search aligned with SHmax would be more physically motivated but requires regional stress orientation data.
  • Wellhead pressure as mediator: WHP is a poor proxy for pore pressure at fault depth (8-10 km). The NDE/NIE decomposition reflects measurement limitations, not physics. Downhole pressure gauges would improve the mediation analysis.
  • No time-varying confounding model: Operators adjust injection rates in response to seismicity (feedback loop). Our cross-sectional TMLE does not model this time-varying confounding. Longitudinal TMLE (LTMLE) would address this but requires significant methodological development.
  • CV-TMLE not yet deployed: Cross-validated TMLE was implemented but the histogram density estimator is unstable under fold-wise sample splitting. Standard TMLE with H-truncation is used instead. Kernel density estimation would fix this.
  • No HAL base learner: The Highly Adaptive Lasso (van der Laan's recommended learner for theoretical convergence guarantees) is R-only. Adding it requires an R bridge.
  • Detection bias: TexNet's earthquake detection threshold varies spatially. Wells in well-monitored areas may show more small events. No spatial detection-bias adjustment is currently applied.
  • Formation data dropped: Operator-reported formation labels are unreliable. The depth-class proxy captures the same physical distinction but sensitivity analysis shows the direct effect is sensitive to this choice (±50-950% at some radii). The indirect (pressure) pathway is robust (±7-18%).
